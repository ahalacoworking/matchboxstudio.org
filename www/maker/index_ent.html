HISTORY OF ENTREPRENEURSHIP

The modern era of entrepreneurship has evolved since the term was introduced by French economist Jean-Baptiste Say, in the early 1800s. Say’s focus was on the OPTIMIZATION of resources as a result of shifting them from lower to higher areas of productivity; a definition that would be fitting of a wide array of entrepreneurial endeavors even today. In the 20th century, Austrian-American economist and political scientist Joseph Schumpeter focused his definition of entrepreneurship on the role of DISRUPTION, or “creative destruction,” as the primary driver in economic growth.

Later that century, Peter Drucker, an American management consultant and educator, focused on entrepreneurs as best defined by those who spot and exploit OPPORTUNITY in order to be successful. Howard Stevenson, a contemporary Harvard School of Business professor, has more recently expounded on the RESOURCEFULNESS of the entrepreneur as their most distinguishing trait, which doesn’t allow resources to limit their options.

 

The authors of this paper would like to contribute to this great history of entrepreneurship by adding that VISION may be the most useful trait of a modern entrepreneur as it considers the motivation of the entrepreneur; the critical element of pursuing entrepreneurial activity not as much out of what could be, but more of a fixing of the present in light of a future picture of “the way that things should be.”

As the definition has evolved, it has grown from a simple umbrella term that identifies any individual who can optimize resources to a more focused, opportunistic and innovative person with a desire to deconstruct the status quo to solve a problem. In other words, the shift from a Small and Medium Enterprise focus to an Innovation Driven Enterprise focus is clear, and mirrors the evolution of the entrepreneurial culture across the country and, more importantly, in Greater Lafayette.

Innovation is the specific instrument of entrepreneurship... the act that endows resources with a new capacity to create wealth.
-Drucker

SMEs COMPARED TO IDEs

We believe that promoting a general understanding of the difference between Small and Medium Enterprises (SMEs) and Innovation Driven Enterprises (IDEs) is vital in cultivating a community that embraces the rapidly changing world of entrepreneurship. Key areas of differentiation include market opportunities, funding options, response to capital, growth potential, and job opportunities.

 

The Kauffman Foundation’s recently published report entitled “The Tale of Two Entrepreneurs” draws a clear and important distinction between SMEs and IDEs, or small businesses and startups, and why it’s important for governments and policymakers to support both.

 

Tippecanoe County scored a 99.8 Innovation Index score in the Economic Development Administration ratings, third behind Kosciusko County (107.9) and Hamilton County (100.7). The index factors in human capital, economic dynamics, productivity and employment, and economic well-being.

 

Business has only two basic functions-marketing and innovation.

Market Opportunity

From a market opportunity perspective, SMEs tend to focus on a local market. For example, a retail operation or dining establishment caters to the needs of the potential custom- ers that are in their direct proximity. Conversely, IDEs tend to have global opportunities in a very niche market. Consider high-tech intellectual property, such as a cloud-based data software product, that can significantly impact or alter an industry or series of industries around the globe, and thereby access global markets and leapfrog revenue projections confined to a local geography.

Jobs

The types of jobs that are created by SMEs are generally performed locally and may or may not be able to transfer to other industries. Job creation by IDEs, though, often results in tradeable opportunities for a global workforce wherein jobs can be performed remotely anywhere in the world. The necessary skills for these positions are frequently transferable as well. An individual who writes software in the agricultural industry can often do the same in another industry—education, manufacturing, etc.

Entrepreneurship is the relentless pursuit of opportunity without regard to resources currently controlled.
-Stevenson

Funding

For an SME, traditional funding options are relatively straightforward: banks provide small business loans to individuals or partnerships that are deemed low-risk, based on a trusted formulaic analysis that’s been improved over time. These same risk management calculations don’t work in favor of IDEs, as they often have a period of latency after funding from research and development. The acceptance of failure in the support of IDEs is critical to encourage this type of startup activity to thrive.

Funding options for IDEs, beyond the simple but effective “boot strapping,” can be nondilutive long-in- vestment interest in the form of grant opportunities from sources like SBIR/STTRs from Departments of Agriculture, Commerce, Defense, Education, Health and Human Services, Homeland Security, Interi- or, Transportation, EPA, NASA, and especially the NSF, as well as other national and more regional organizations, or dilutive with investment opportuni- ties from common places such as angel investors and venture capital (VC) firms.

 

It is the authors’ firm belief that a disciplined focus on the quality of the startup community itself, with a long-term perspective, is the best way to attract start-up capital. Regionally, sources of investment capital currently available to IDEs are limited but increasing. The Purdue Foundry, in partnership with Elevate Ventures and the Indiana Economic Development Corporation, operate the Elevate Purdue Foundry Fund.

VisionTech Partners, an Indiana based group with an angel investment arm, operates regionally and has a specific chapter dedicated to the Greater Lafayette area. M25 Group and Hyde Park Angels both operate out of the Chicago area with interest in Greater Lafayette. MatchBOX supports investment groups that can make an impact in our community and give IDEs investment options to help propel their startups forward.

Summary

A single-word approach to summarize the variances between the two categories of entrepreneurial activity is to refer to SMEs, or traditional small businesses, as evolutionary. The growth of the organization is steady and consistent, but can be slow and limited. When ran well, they can continue and do well for generations, but change is often slow. Conversely, IDEs, or startups, are revolutionary. They’re disruptive by nature, and albeit volatile, they can cause rapid and immediate growth and change.

That being said, it’s critical for communities, policymakers, and legislation to support both types of entrepreneurship. The authors of this paper and MatchBOX support policies and programs that remove hurdles for entrepreneurs and allow for the forward-movement of SMEs and IDEs alike. Examples include recent federal legislation that further expands crowdfunding, Entrepreneurship Week, Xtern program - an immersive summer tech internship experience, and the XPat program - an effort to draw skilled workers back to Indiana. Alignment with these existing efforts is a logical and strategic possibility for the Greater Lafayette community.

We believe building a community based on the optimism of a better future, a shared vision for collaboration and ideation, a culture of openness, surrounded by innovation-driven service providers, and favorable policies is a necessary compliment to the existing business climate and a recipe for a healthy and thriving Greater Lafayette.
